✓ What to Do
  • Cut weak opps early — no problem, no buyer, no next step means it shouldn't be in your pipeline.
  • Force clarity after discovery: lock in the use case, buying trigger, and decision path.
  • Use one-to-many activities only when they create real, measurable movement.
✓ What to Do
  • Design every demo to unlock a decision — not just to inform.
  • Attack stalled deals head-on: advance them, rescope, or close them out.
  • Be ruthless about priorities: highest ACV, earliest close, highest likelihood to close.
✓ What to Do
  • Make value promises explicit at handover — don't leave post-sales guessing.
  • Re-engage only when there's a real new pain or expansion trigger.
✓ What to Do
  • Run expansion discovery only when there's a real business trigger behind it.
  • Enable partners only when it leads to real, qualified opportunities.
✗ What to Avoid
  • Don't keep "nice conversations" alive as pipeline — they're not opportunities.
  • Don't run discovery with no decision outcome in mind.
  • Don't create pipeline without clear Sales ownership.
✗ What to Avoid
  • Don't spend time on deals with no active buyer.
  • Don't over-engineer low-probability deals — the juice isn't worth the squeeze.
  • Don't treat all pipeline as equal — not every deal deserves the same attention.
✗ What to Avoid
  • Don't stay involved in pure delivery — that's not your lane.
  • Don't attend QBRs without a clear expansion intent.
✗ What to Avoid
  • Don't push innovation without a buying trigger to back it up.
  • Don't run upsell motions without AE/CSM alignment.
🗝️ The 3-Question Pipeline Test
  • Will this move the deal forward this quarter?
  • Will this improve pipeline quality or conversion?
  • Will this remove weak pipeline if needed?
If no — don't engage.
Bottom Line
Pipeline Progression gets better when you help deals move — or disappear. Keeping weak pipeline alive hurts conversion, focus, and your variable.
